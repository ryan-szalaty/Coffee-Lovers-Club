Our Guide to Making Good Coffee

"All you need is hot water and a cup. And maybe a shot of espresso." - Ryan Szalaty

Espresso

Espresso is a concentrated coffee beverage made by forcing hot water through finely-ground coffee beans. It's known for its strong flavor and relatively small serving size.

Espresso is typically made using an espresso machine, where water is pressurized and forced through a "shot" of finely-ground coffee beans. The result is a small, intense shot of coffee with a layer of crema on top. If you watch a video on how it's made well, you'll see a nectar-like orange liquid being filtered into a glass.

Americano

An Americano is a milder coffee beverage that is essentially a diluted espresso. It has a flavor profile closer to drip coffee but with the strength of espresso.

To make an Americano, you simply add hot water to a shot of espresso. The amount of water added can be adjusted to your preference, resulting in a weaker or stronger Americano.

Cappuccino

A cappuccino is a coffee drink that consists of equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam. It's known for its balanced and creamy taste.

To make a cappuccino, an espresso shot is poured into a cup, followed by an equal amount of steamed milk. The top is then adorned with a thick layer of milk foam, which is often shaped into decorative patterns.

Latte

A latte is a milder coffee drink made with espresso and a larger amount of steamed milk than a cappuccino. It's known for its smooth and creamy texture.

A latte starts with a shot of espresso, which is then topped with a larger amount of steamed milk and a small amount of milk foam.

Mocha

A mocha is a coffee drink that combines espresso with hot chocolate, resulting in a sweet and chocolatey coffee experience.

Mochas typically start with a shot of espresso, which is mixed with hot chocolate or cocoa powder. Steamed milk is then added, and the drink is often topped with whipped cream and chocolate shavings.

Macchiato

"Macchiato" means "stained" or "spotted" in Italian. A macchiato is a simple coffee drink made by adding a shot of espresso to a small amount of frothy milk.

To make a macchiato, a shot of espresso is "stained" or "marked" with a small dollop of frothy milk. It's a great choice if you enjoy the strength of espresso but want a touch of milk.

Ristretto

Ristretto is an even more concentrated version of espresso. It's made with the same amount of coffee but with less water, resulting in a shorter shot with a richer, more intense flavor.

Ristretto is prepared like espresso but with a shorter extraction time, using less water. This yields a smaller, more concentrated shot of coffee.

Flat White

A flat white is a coffee drink that originated in Australia and New Zealand. It's similar to a latte but with a velvety microfoam milk texture and a strong coffee flavor.

A flat white is made with a shot of espresso and steamed milk. The key difference is the texture of the milk, which should have a smooth, velvety microfoam rather than a thick layer of foam.

Affogato

An affogato is a simple yet delightful dessert made by pouring a shot of hot espresso over a scoop of vanilla ice cream or gelato.

To make an affogato, a scoop of vanilla ice cream or gelato is placed in a serving glass or dish, and a shot of hot espresso is poured over it. The espresso both melts the ice cream and blends with it, creating a rich and contrasting combination of hot and cold, bitter and sweet.

Vienna Coffee

Vienna coffee is a luxurious coffee drink that includes coffee, whipped cream, and sometimes chocolate or cocoa powder.

A Vienna coffee starts with a shot of espresso, which is topped with a generous dollop of whipped cream. Some versions also include a dusting of cocoa powder or grated chocolate for added flavor.

Milk Foam: What to Know

By using a steaming wand, hot air is introduced to the liquid, expanding it's volume and creating bubbles (AKA froth). The milk is heated to a specific temperature, typically between 150째F to 170째F (65째C to 75째C), which is below boiling point. The exact temperature may vary depending the type of milk being used. What's important to know is that, at such a temperature, the natural sweetness of the milk comes out. That's also why sugar is never added to many coffee drinks.

Technically, froth can be made with any kind of milk, but as a general rule, milk with more fat content is used. This is because the fats produce a rich and creamy foam and a velvetty texture more easily.

You can make froth using any of the following tools:

  • Steam Wand: In commercial coffee machines, a steam wand is used to inject steam into the milk, creating foam as the milk expands.
  • Handheld Frother: For home use, handheld milk frothers or frothing wands are commonly used. These devices whisk and aerate the milk to produce foam.
  • French Press: Simple, yet effective home tool that contains a plunger to pump coffee or milk. To mimic the introduction of air through a steam wand, people pump the plunger several times.

How to Make the Perfect Milk Foam

It's no surprise that making milk foam is a challenge for the beginning coffee connoisseur. Without foam, many espresso-based drinks lose their charm and personality. Here are the steps for making creamy, sweet foam each and every time.

For French press users:

  1. Choose a milk of your preference (fattier milk is best) and fill your pitcher about 3/4 of the way with milk. When introducing air, we are effectively expanding the milk, so you will have room and prevent spils.
  2. Either on the stovetop or using a steam wand, heat the milk to around 150째F to 170째F (65째C to 75째C). Thermometers are really handy to get precise temperature measurements, but as a general rule, once you see some smoke, it's time to remove the milk from the heat. Steam wands take care of this for you.
  3. Use the hand plunger until satisfied with the milk texture. If, however, you are using a steam wand, read further down about properly introducing air.

For steam wand users:

  1. Angle the pitcher at a 30-degree angle with the spout facing slightly down. Insert the tip of the wand into the milk. Most, but not all of the tip should be submerged into the milk. Done correctly, this will create a whirlpool effect that moves air around the milk and generates great foam.
  2. Start your steam wand, gradually increasing the pressure until you have a decent amount of movement. You can move the pitcher slighly, but it should not be necessary.
  3. Listen to the sound being made. Well-done frothing should be relatively quiet and sound like pressurized liquid. If your milk is making a very high-pitched noise during frothing, your wand tip is too high.
  4. Determine the temperature of the milk by placing one hand on the bottom of the pitcher. Once the bottom of the pitcher is too hot to handle, the milk is ready.
  5. Once satisfied with the froth level, turn off the steam wand and wait for a few seconds. Carefully remove the pitcher and wipe the steam wand tip with a wet towel. Clear the space around the wand and let out some steam once more. This is to remove and residual milk from the wand.

Espresso: What to Know

Espresso is a concentrated coffee beverage that is brewed by forcing hot water through finely-ground coffee beans. In other words, there is a large amount of flavor compounds that get extracted through hot water, producing a strong, caffeine-packed liquid. A single shot of espresso, 1oz, contains roughly 30 milligrams of caffeine, while a standard 8oz cup of coffee has 95-165 milligrams.

Adding an espresso shot to a cup of coffee can add up to over 200 milligrams of caffeine in a single serving depending on the size of the shot. Generally, you can consume up to 400 milligrams in one day without any issues. With that in mind, please drink responsibly!

Crema, a caramel-colored liquid that is made when extracting espresso, is the golden standard for flavor quality. It is formed when carbon dioxide gas is released during the brewing process and combines with coffee oils to create a frothy layer. Generating crema requires well-compacted and fine coffee grounds filtered through hot water.

For espresso, you will need the following tools:

  • Espresso Machine: Espresso machines are different from typical coffee machines in that they do not use a pot. Instead, they use a portafilter that inserts into the machine just below a hot water dispenser. These machines should come with their own portafilter.
  • Portafilter: These look like handles with a section to insert a small steel coffee filter. Coffee grounds are compacted (or tamped) into the filters directly, which are then inserted into the handle.
  • Tamper: These are used to compact the grounds into a dense layer so that water is run through evenly. They are quite heavy and come as a set with an espresso machine.

How to Make the Perfect Espresso

Making espresso perfectly can be challenging, especially for newcomers to the craft. Take a look at our guide below:

  1. Ensure that your coffee grounds are quite fine: They should be close to powder consistency. You can either purchase finely-grated grounds or make them yourself using an electric grinder. Any grounds that come out too thick can easily be used for regular coffee, so no need to fret about this step.
  2. Place coffee grounds into the portafilter and use your tamper to compress the grounds evenly. This will ensure that no pockets of air are present for water to escape. Water moves through the area of least resistance, so any uneven tamping will result in an uneven flavor extraction. Practice is key!
  3. When satisfied, place the portafilter into the machine and turn it on. Make sure you have a shot glass or something similar that can catch the liquid. If your liquid comes out evenly with a caramelly brown or orange color, then congratulations! You have made the perfect espresso.
  4. Crema only lasts for about 30 seconds as carbon dioxide combines with the coffee oils. For that reason, be sure to use it immediately. Using new coffee grounds is always best as the oils will be fresh.
